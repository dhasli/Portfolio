16TB Gone in One Click

How a bank lost 16 terabytes of 7-year compliance data overnight — and how I went back in time to find out why.

Shibu A ⏱ 8 min read  ·  Technical Story
Chapter 1

☕ A Normal Day That Wasn't

Coffee. Tickets. The usual hum of a support desk doing its thing.

Then my colleague picks up a case. Routine check — bank client, enterprise backup environment, Dell EMC NetWorker. He stares at the case description for a second, then reads it again.

"16TB of backup data is missing."

Not failed. Not corrupted. Not pending. Missing.

Now if you know NetWorker even a little, you know that word doesn't belong in the same sentence as it. NetWorker doesn't just wake up on a Tuesday and decide to delete data. That's not how it works. That's not how any of this works.

But try explaining that to a top-tier bank that's already halfway through drafting an escalation email.

🏦
The Stakes
This wasn't a startup or some small business. This was one of India's top banks. The backup in question was for a critical application — the kind you can't take offline for a cold backup. 7-year retention. Compliance requirement. Banking regulation. The whole works.

The workflow had been running clean for months. Backup triggers, data goes to Data Domain, clone goes to tape, everything happy. Until one day the backup admin notices something very, very wrong.

🚨
The Red Flag
A tape belonging to this workflow was being relabeled. The admin caught it mid-process and pulled the emergency brake — stopped the backup job immediately.

Relabeling a tape means one thing: the system believes the data on it is no longer needed. It's essentially saying "this tape is free real estate." And in this case, that "free real estate" held 16TB of 7-year compliance data.

The admin panicked. Stopped the job. Escalation started. My colleague took the case.

Chapter 2

🕵️ Taking Ownership of a Mystery

My colleague did the initial analysis. Checked Action logs. Checked the media database. Cross-referenced everything. There was no trace of why this happened. The logs confidently stated:

NetWorker mminfo output
$ mminfo -a -q "name=/critical_app_backup"
mminfo: no matches found for the query

# Backup/clone completed with 2months/7years retention. But deleted within a month.
# Nothing suggests this data should have been removed.
# And yet... the tape is being relabeled.

The escalation was already brewing. The customer was fully convinced NetWorker had a bug. They had every receipt — the backup completed, the clone completed, logs showed 7-year retention. And yet the data was gone and the tape was being recycled.

I jumped in and took ownership of the case.

Not because I had answers. But because nobody did, and someone had to.

I set two goals. In order:

  1. Recover as much data as possible. The bank needed that data. 7 years of compliance data doesn't just disappear without consequence.
  2. Find the actual root cause. Because "I don't know why this happened" is not something you say to a bank twice.

In the backup world, a saveset is like a book. If someone rips out the last chapter, you don't have a complete book anymore. Standard restore? Won't work. The system knows the saveset is incomplete and refuses to restore it cleanly.

It's like a key with half its teeth missing. It exists. But it won't open anything useful.

Chapter 4

💡 The Tool I Underestimated: UASM

I had used UASM before. But only for basic stuff — controlling backup or clone actions from CLI. I never pushed it. Never needed to.

This time I had no choice. Standard tools weren't going to cut it.

🔧
What is UASM?
UASM (User-mode Application Specific Module) is a NetWorker CLI utility that gives you raw, low-level control over backup and recovery operations — bypassing the usual metadata requirements. It doesn't care about pretty, complete savesets. It just pulls data directly.

Here's the plan I proposed:

01
Retrieve Tape 1 from cold storage
The ejected tape was physically stored offsite. We needed it back in the library — but not mounted yet, just loaded.
02
Scan the saveset index
Run a scanner against the tape to rebuild whatever index metadata we could, even with the tail missing.
03
UASM direct recovery to storage node
Use the storage node CLI to run UASM recovery — extracting data directly to local disk, ignoring the missing tail.
04
Backup recovered data back to tape
Create a temporary workflow, back up the recovered data from disk to a fresh tape with proper 7-year retention. Data lives again.
🍀
Lucky break
The storage node had ~20TB of free disk space. Without that, this entire plan collapses. Sometimes the universe cooperates.
Storage Node CLI — UASM Recovery
# Step 1: Load tape without mounting
$ nsrjb -l -S TAPE_VOL_001
Tape loaded successfully.

# Step 2: Scan saveset index
$ scanner -v -S TAPE_VOL_001
Scanning volume TAPE_VOL_001...
Found saveset: ssid=XXXXXXX [partial - tail missing]

# Step 3: UASM recovery to local path
$ uasm -r -f /recover/critical_app/ -v
Starting recovery from tape...
Recovering chunk 1/1024...
Recovering chunk 512/1024...
Recovering chunk 1024/1024...
Recovery complete: 15,360 GB restored to /recover/critical_app/

Slowly, data started coming back. No dramatic fanfare. Just steady progress — chunk by chunk, the 15TB reappearing on disk. We then backed it up to a new tape with correct retention.

Not perfect. The 1TB tail was gone forever. But no longer catastrophic.

Recovery ✅ done. Now the harder question: why did this happen at all?
Chapter 5

⏳ "Go Back in Time" — Seriously?

I reached out to the L3 SMEs. Explained the situation. Asked for an action plan.

Their response?

"Go to the past. Check when the data actually expired."

I stared at that for a moment. Go to the past. Right. Very helpful. Thanks L3.

Except — they weren't being philosophical. They were talking about Bootstrap Backups.

🧠
What is a Bootstrap Backup?
A Bootstrap is NetWorker's self-backup — it runs every day and captures the server's entire brain: media database, resource files, auth config. It's what you use to rebuild a NetWorker server from scratch after a disaster. But here's the thing — it's also a time machine. Every day's bootstrap = a snapshot of what NetWorker thought was true on that date.

We pulled a full month of bootstrap backups. Restored them all into an isolated folder. Took them back to my own lab. Then replayed the media database state — day by day — watching for when things changed.

Day 1: retention correct. Day 5: correct. Day 10: correct. Day 20: correct. Day 24: correct. Day 25…

Day 25: The savesets were marked as expired.

Weeks before they should have been. Something changed on Day 25.

Chapter 6

🧨 The Truth Nobody Wanted to Hear

Audit logs. That's where we went next.

NetWorker keeps detailed audit logs of every action taken — who did what, when, from which IP, on which saveset.

NetWorker Audit Log — Day 25
$ nsr_audit_log -s nsrserver -t "DD/MM/YY 00:00:00" -e "DD+1/MM/YY 23:59:59"

DD/MM/YY 14:32:17 user: backup_admin@bankhost
action: modify saveset retention
ssid: XXXXXXX (critical_app_backup)
old retention: 7 years
new retention: Expire Immediately

# Also affected: 4 other savesets in same session
# User action: UI dropdown — selected wrong option

There it was.

The customer had intended to extend the retention on some savesets. The UI has a dropdown. They opened it. They saw the options. And instead of picking a longer date… they clicked "Expire Immediately."

One click.

No warning popup. No "are you sure you want to delete 16TB of banking compliance data?" confirmation dialog. Just — done.

Zero bugs. Zero NetWorker issues. 100% by-design behavior — executing exactly what it was told to do.

This is the part where you have to deliver the news carefully. Because technically you're about to tell a bank:

"You deleted your own data. NetWorker was innocent the whole time."

We presented the evidence. Audit logs, bootstrap timeline, the whole story. It was received… professionally. Quietly. The kind of quiet that means someone is having a very bad internal conversation.

Chapter 7

🧯 The 45-Day Cleanup

This wasn't the end. We now had to make sure the same mistake hadn't happened to other savesets. The customer had changed retention on multiple savesets that day.

So we went through every tape in that library that might be affected. Loaded them, ran the scanner, checked retention values, corrected them back to compliance dates. Tape by tape. Saveset by saveset.

45

15TB
Data recovered
1
Wrong click. That's all it took.

Long process. Necessary process. Every tape had to be verified. Every retention date had to match compliance requirements. We rebuilt what we could, documented what we couldn't, and made sure it wouldn't silently fail again.

Final Thoughts

🎯 What This Case Changed for Me

Technically, this was a fascinating case. UASM recovery of a tailless saveset. Time-travel debugging via bootstrap history. Audit log forensics. All great stuff to add to the toolbelt.

But the real lesson wasn't technical.

💬
The real lesson:
Backup systems don't fail dramatically. They fail quietly — when a small mistake slips through and nobody notices until it's too late. NetWorker didn't delete 16TB of data. It followed instructions. A human made the decision. The system did exactly what it was told.

And ever since that case — without exception — whenever someone says "we didn't change anything," my immediate next move is to check the audit logs. Because someone always changed something.

Always.
